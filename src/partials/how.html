How does it work?

Transport Analyses

At every street junction in a city, UTA measures travel times to every other point in the city with any possible combination of active and public transport (such as walking, cycling, bus, tram, train). These times are then converted into ratios relative to equivalent travel times with private automobiles.

Lower ratios indicate that active and public transport are faster than private automobiles. Ratios also generally increase with distance. For each point in a city, UTA measures the average ratio out to some fixed distance, such as 5km.

Socio-demographic Analyses

The transport index described in the adjacent panel is then combined with socio-demographic indices such as indices of deprivation or unemployment statistics. These statistics are standardised and multiplied by the transport index, to generate a compound "UTA index." The socio-demographic statistics are standardised so that the scale of the index is determined only by the scale of the transport index. This means that UTA index values can be meaningfully compared between cities.

UTA index

A low UTA index value reflects good access to transport infrastructure and desirable measures of the chosen socio-demographic statistic. A high UTA index values reflects poor access to transport in combination with undesirable socio-demographic statistics.
